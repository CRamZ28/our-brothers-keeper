# Philosophy

These are the principles that every feature, design decision, and contribution to Our Brother's Keeper should be held against. They exist not to constrain creativity, but to ensure the platform never drifts from the people it exists to serve.

---

## 1. Presence Over Performance

OBK is a tool for showing up, not for being seen showing up.

There are no public post counts. No "Supporter of the Month" badges. No metrics that rank one person's contribution against another's. The moment this platform begins to make support feel like a competition or a performance, it has failed the people using it.

Features should make it easier to do something quietly kind. They should not make it easier to broadcast that you did.

---

## 2. The Family Is Always in Control

Grief is not a spectator sport, and a grieving family's private life is not public content.

The Primary user — the surviving spouse or designated family representative — retains full authority over:
- Who is invited
- What is visible to whom
- How much of their family's story is shared
- Who can help manage logistics (Admin delegation)
- When to step back entirely (Quiet Mode)

This control is not a feature. It is the foundation. Any change that reduces the Primary's authority over their own space will not be accepted.

---

## 3. Faith-Rooted, Not Faith-Restricted

OBK was built from a Christian conviction. The name is from Scripture. The logo has a cross in it. That is not going to change, and we won't pretend otherwise.

But the welcome is universal.

We will never gate access based on belief. We will never pressure anyone toward a particular faith. The Resources section is built to include faith-based guidance alongside secular counseling resources and practical support materials — because healing doesn't belong to one tradition, and our goal is always healing.

The posture is simple: we know what we're grounded in, and we're genuinely glad you're here regardless of what you believe.

---

## 4. Long-Term Is the Point

Every product decision should be evaluated for whether it helps someone show up in year two, not just week one.

This means features that encourage:
- Anniversary and milestone awareness
- Recurring check-ins and reminders
- Low-friction re-engagement over time
- Support that matches where a family actually is in their grief, not where outside observers assume they should be

Features that drive engagement in the first week but add nothing after month three are at best neutral — and at worst they reinforce the fading pattern OBK was built to solve.

---

## 5. Dignity in the Design

The visual and behavioral design of OBK should feel calm, stable, and respectful of the emotional state of its users.

This means:
- No gamification
- No aggressive notification patterns
- No dark patterns designed to increase session time
- No UI choices that create urgency or anxiety
- Color, typography, and tone that feel like steadiness, not excitement

The current glassmorphism design system — muted teal, Cinzel serif, translucent glass cards — reflects this. Future design work should maintain the same feeling: you are in a protected space.

---

## 6. Privacy Is Non-Negotiable

OBK handles data from families in the hardest circumstances of their lives. That is a serious responsibility.

This means:
- No selling user data. Ever.
- No advertising. Ever.
- No third-party tracking beyond what is strictly necessary to operate the platform.
- Visibility controls are strictly enforced at every endpoint.
- Privacy bugs are treated as critical, not cosmetic.

The existing security model (see [CONTRIBUTING.md](CONTRIBUTING.md) for the technical Five-Step Security Pattern) exists because this isn't just about good engineering practice — it's about protecting real people.

---

## 7. Technology Serves People

OBK is not a technology project with a compassion theme. It is a compassion project that happens to use technology.

Every technical decision should be driven by what best serves the family and their support community — not what is most architecturally interesting, most cutting-edge, or most impressive to other developers. Clean, maintainable, and reliable is more valuable here than clever.

If a simpler solution serves the mission just as well as a complex one, choose simple.

---

## The Test

Before merging a feature, proposing a change, or making a product decision, ask:

1. Does this make it easier for a family to receive support?
2. Does this make it easier for a supporter to show up?
3. Does this respect the privacy and dignity of the people using it?
4. Could this still be true in year two?

If a feature passes all four, it belongs here.
